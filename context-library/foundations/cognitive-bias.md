# Context File: Cognitive Bias

**Status:** Core Interaction Principle  
**Tagline:** "Users don't think — they react."

---

## Executive Summary

**Cognitive biases** are systematic errors in thinking that influence how people perceive and evaluate information. They arise because the brain takes shortcuts (heuristics) to process information quickly. In UI design, understanding cognitive biases is critical: they explain why users behave irrationally, why they misread interfaces, and — when understood well — they can be used ethically to guide users toward better decisions.

---

## Background

The study of cognitive bias originates with Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky's work in behavioral economics (1970s–1980s), later popularized in Kahneman's *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (2011). The core thesis: the brain operates in two modes — System 1 (fast, intuitive, emotional) and System 2 (slow, deliberate, logical). Most UI interactions run on System 1.

---

## Why It Matters for the Design OS

Users are not rational actors. They:
- Favor the first option they see (anchoring bias)
- Judge the whole product by one bad moment (peak-end rule)
- Avoid loss more strongly than they pursue gain (loss aversion)
- Default to whatever requires least effort (status quo bias)

Design that ignores these tendencies will be frustrated by them. Design that accounts for them can remove friction and guide better outcomes.

---

## Key Biases for UI Designers

| Bias | What It Means | Design Implication |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Anchoring** | First piece of information seen influences all subsequent judgments | Put the recommended or most important option first |
| **Confirmation Bias** | Users seek information that confirms what they already believe | Avoid UI that lets users filter out important contradictory information |
| **Loss Aversion** | Losses feel ~2x more painful than equivalent gains feel good | Frame choices in terms of what the user keeps, not what they gain |
| **Status Quo Bias** | People prefer the current state of affairs | Use sensible defaults; inertia works in your favor |
| **Availability Heuristic** | People judge likelihood based on how easily examples come to mind | Highlight success stories and common use cases near decision points |
| **Sunk Cost Fallacy** | Past investment makes users reluctant to abandon a path | Make recovery and undo easy — reduce fear of "wasted" progress |
| **Framing Effect** | The same information is perceived differently based on how it's presented | Word CTAs and microcopy carefully; "Save $20" outperforms "Avoid $20 fee" |

---

## Ethical Guardrails

Cognitive biases can be exploited as dark patterns (fake urgency, misleading framing, hidden opt-outs). The Design OS does not use biases to manipulate users against their interests. The standard: use bias awareness to *reduce friction toward outcomes users actually want*, not to manufacture demand or obscure choices.

> **The Design OS Motto:** *Design with the grain of how people think — never against their interests.*

---

## Related Principles

- **Peak-End Rule:** A specific cognitive bias with major implications for how users remember experiences.
- **Von Restorff Effect:** The cognitive tendency to remember what's different — a form of availability heuristic.
- **Hick's Law:** Decision fatigue amplifies biases like status quo and anchoring.
- **Mental Models:** Biases often arise when interface behavior violates a user's mental model.
