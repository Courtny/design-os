# Context File: Peak-End Rule

**Status:** Core Interaction Principle  
**Tagline:** "Users remember the worst moment and the last moment — nothing else."

---

## Executive Summary

The **Peak-End Rule** is a cognitive bias in which people judge an experience primarily based on how they felt at its most intense point (the "peak," positive or negative) and at its conclusion (the "end"), rather than the average or total of all moments within it. For product designers, this is one of the most actionable memory-and-emotion principles available: it means that a well-designed ending and carefully managed worst-case moments can dramatically improve how users remember an experience — even if much of the middle was mediocre.

---

## The Origin Story

Psychologists Daniel Kahneman, Barbara Fredrickson, and colleagues documented the peak-end rule in the early 1990s through studies on pain and medical procedures. In one striking study, patients who underwent a colonoscopy rated the experience as significantly better when the procedure ended with a period of mild discomfort (rather than sharp pain), even though this extended the total duration of the procedure. The end quality mattered more than total duration.

---

## Why It Matters for the Design OS

1. **The last screen in a flow shapes how users remember the whole experience.** A clunky confirmation page after a smooth checkout flow leaves a worse memory than the checkout deserved. A delightful success state can redeem a frustrating flow.
2. **Worst moments are memorable, not average moments.** A single confusing error state that users hit repeatedly will define their memory of the product more than dozens of smooth interactions that preceded it.
3. **Duration neglect is real.** Users don't calculate the average quality of their experience. A 5-minute flow with one great ending is remembered more fondly than a 2-minute flow that ends with friction.

---

## Implementation Guidelines

| Focus Area | Application |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Design success states with care** | End screens — order confirmation, upload complete, onboarding done — deserve as much design attention as any other screen. Celebrate the completion. |
| **Audit for worst-case moments** | Identify the highest-friction, most error-prone, or most frustrating step in any flow. This is the "peak" users will remember. Fix it first. |
| **End error states well** | If a user experiences an error, the error recovery path is the "end" of that experience. A clear, friendly recovery matters as much as the error message. |
| **Onboarding end state** | The last step of onboarding should feel like arrival, not just the end of setup. Give users a clear sense that they're ready to go. |
| **Email and notification endings** | Transactional emails end at the final line. Make sure it's useful or affirming, not boilerplate legal copy. |

---

## The Implication for Feedback Timing

If you're collecting satisfaction feedback (NPS, CSAT), the timing matters enormously because of the Peak-End Rule. Survey right after a peak or a positive end state and ratings will be higher. Survey after a frustrating support interaction and they'll be lower — even if the preceding product experience was good. Be aware of when you're measuring and what you're actually capturing.

> **The Design OS Motto:** *Nail the ending. Fix the worst moment. The rest takes care of itself.*

---

## Related Principles

- **Cognitive Bias:** The Peak-End Rule is one of the most consequential biases for experience design.
- **Goal-Gradient Effect:** The "end" is the finish line — combining peak-end attention with goal-gradient momentum creates powerful endings.
- **Flow:** Disruptions to flow create negative peaks. Sustained flow with a strong ending produces excellent recall.
- **Aesthetic-Usability Effect:** A polished ending screen amplifies positive peak-end memory.
